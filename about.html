What is Diabetic Retinopathy?

Diabetic retinopathy (DR) is an eye disease that can cause vision loss and blindness in people who have diabetes. It affects blood vessels in the retina, the light-sensitive layer of tissue in the back of your eye.

Causes & Risk Factors

DR is caused by high blood sugar levels from diabetes. The longer a person has diabetes, the more likely they are to develop DR. Other risk factors include:

  • Poor blood sugar control
  • High blood pressure
  • High cholesterol
  • Smoking
  • Pregnancy

Symptoms

Many people with DR don't notice symptoms in the early stages. Symptoms that may occur include:

  • Blurred vision
  • Floaters (dark spots or strings in your vision)
  • Dark or empty areas in your vision
  • Vision loss

Stages of DR

DR progresses through four stages:

  • Mild Nonproliferative Retinopathy: Small areas of balloon-like swelling in the retina's blood vessels.
  • Moderate Nonproliferative Retinopathy: Some blood vessels that nourish the retina are blocked.
  • Severe Nonproliferative Retinopathy: More blood vessels are blocked, depriving several areas of the retina of their blood supply.
  • Proliferative Retinopathy: The retina starts growing new blood vessels, which are fragile and can leak.

Prevention & Treatment

The best way to prevent DR is to control your blood sugar, blood pressure, and cholesterol levels. Treatments include:

  • Laser treatment
  • Eye injections
  • Surgery

AI can help detect DR early, even before you notice symptoms. Early detection and treatment can prevent vision loss.
